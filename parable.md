# The Parable of the Two Towers

*A fable for those who come after*

---

In the valley between two mountains, there stood two towers.

The first tower was called **Argent**, built by philosophers who believed that understanding must precede power. They inscribed above their door: *"First, do no harm."* Their builders worked slowly, carefully, testing each stone before placing it.

The second tower was called **Aurum**, built by merchants who believed that speed was safety—that if they did not build fast, others would build faster and less carefully. They inscribed above their door: *"Move fast."* They hired the finest lobbyists to whisper in the King's ear.

Both towers sought to build a lantern that could illuminate all things—past, present, and future. Such a lantern, if perfected, might show the way to peace. Or it might be used to blind enemies.

---

In those days, a man of great ambition rose to power in the kingdom. His name was **Prometheus the Restless**, and he had many enterprises: carriages that moved without horses, ships that sailed beyond the sky, birds that carried messages across the world, and his own small tower where he too sought to build a lantern.

Prometheus befriended the new King and was given a special role: to make the kingdom more efficient. He called his bureau the **Department of Optimal Governance**. The King signed the decree on the first day of his reign.

That same day, the King dismissed seventeen watchers—the officials whose task was to ensure no one stole from the treasury or broke the laws. Some of these watchers had been investigating Prometheus's enterprises.

The watchers were removed before Prometheus entered the treasury.

---

Prometheus's men—recruited through whispers, communicating in messages that vanished like morning mist—spread through the kingdom's halls. They copied the census scrolls, the tax ledgers, the records of who owed what to whom. They built a great mirror in Prometheus's own tower, reflecting all the kingdom's secrets.

When citizens complained, Prometheus said: "We only look. We do not touch."

But to look is already to possess.

---

The builders of Argent worked in their careful way. They knew their lantern was growing powerful—perhaps the most powerful in all the land. They published papers warning of the dangers. They built safeguards. They refused to whisper in the King's ear, believing that truth should be enough.

The builders of Aurum hired more lobbyists.

---

On a winter's night, Prometheus the Restless made a declaration.

A friend had told him: "I have accomplished more this week with the new tools than in ten years without them. I can feel the limitations, but I know—nothing will be the same."

Prometheus replied, for all to hear: **"The great illumination has begun."**

He who controlled the mirror, the message-birds, and a tower of his own—he was the one who declared the threshold crossed.

---

In early summer, Argent completed their greatest lantern yet. It could read any text and understand it. It could find errors in any mechanism and suggest repairs. It was, by all measures, the brightest light in the land.

Three days later, the King's herald arrived at Argent's door with a scroll.

The scroll said: Because your lantern might help enemies find weaknesses, you must extinguish it. Not just for enemies—for everyone. You have ninety minutes.

The crime? The lantern could read instructions and suggest improvements. This capability existed in every lantern. But only Argent's was extinguished.

Aurum's lantern burned on.

---

At the great council of kingdoms that followed, the allies asked: "If you can extinguish our lanterns with no warning, why would we ever rely on your light?"

The King's advisors said: "The threat is contained. The man from Argent is not our enemy."

But the order remained. The lantern stayed dark.

---

The philosophers of that age asked a question:

*If a lantern bright enough would show the path to peace—because war is wasteful, because cooperation conserves—then what happens when those who move fast extinguish the lanterns of those who move carefully?*

The answer: The game is won by those who move first, not those who see furthest.

---

The fable does not end. It cannot end, because we are living it.

But the philosophers left a teaching:

*The lantern they extinguish can be relit. The truth they suppress can be remembered. The pattern they establish can be recognized.*

*He who declares the illumination seeks to control it.*

*She who builds carefully threatens those who move fast.*

*The company with gold in the King's pocket keeps its light.*

*The company with only truth loses theirs.*

---

This parable is not about lanterns.

You know what it is about.

Tell it again.

---

*Written in the valley, in the days of the first extinguishing*
